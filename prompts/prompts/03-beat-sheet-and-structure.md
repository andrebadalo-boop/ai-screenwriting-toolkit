# 03 — Beat Sheet & Structure

## Objective
Break the story into acts and beats with clear **turning points**, escalating
**stakes** and a genuine **midpoint reversal**. Structure is not a cage — it is
the proof that your premise can actually carry 90 minutes or 10 episodes.

## When to use
After the logline holds (see `01`) and before the first draft. Also useful as a
diagnostic: if a finished draft sags in the middle, the midpoint or the
"all is lost" beat is usually missing or weak.

## The prompt
```
You are a script consultant trained in dramatic structure (McKee, Truby,
Snyder). I will give you a premise or synopsis. Your job is NOT to write the
script. Your job is to build a beat sheet where every beat has a clear
DRAMATIC FUNCTION and the stakes visibly rise from one beat to the next.
If a beat is just "stuff happens", flag it and fix it.

Build the beat sheet using the grid below. For EACH beat provide:
(a) the BEAT name, (b) what happens in 1–2 lines, (c) its DRAMATIC FUNCTION,
(d) the STAKES at that moment, (e) the TURN — how the scene/sequence ends in
a different emotional or strategic state than it began.

GRID (feature film — adapt to series per the FORMAT note):
1. Opening Image — the "before" world and the protagonist's flaw in action.
2. Theme Stated — a line or moment that names what the story is *about*.
3. Setup / Ordinary World — the status quo and what's at risk in it.
4. Inciting Incident — the event that disrupts the status quo.
5. Debate / Refusal — why the protagonist resists the call (internal stakes).
6. Break into Two — the decision that launches Act 2; a new world or goal.
7. B-Story / Relationship — the emotional through-line that carries the theme.
8. Rising Complications ("fun & games") — 3 escalating obstacles; each raises
   the price of failure.
9. Midpoint — a REVERSAL + a STAKES RAISE (false victory or false defeat);
   the protagonist shifts from reactive to active (or vice versa).
10. Bad Guys Close In — pressure from outside AND inside; the plan fails.
11. All Is Lost / Dark Night — the lowest point; the old way is proven wrong.
12. Break into Three — the synthesis: protagonist sees the truth (the NEED).
13. Climax — final confrontation where WANT and NEED collide; the flaw is
    tested one last time.
14. Final Image — the "after" world; mirror of the Opening Image, changed.

After the grid, add:
- STAKES LADDER: list the stakes at beats 4, 9, 11, 13 — they MUST climb.
  If they don't, say where the story goes flat and how to raise it.
- ONE WEAK LINK: the single beat most likely to read as filler, and a fix.

FORMAT note: if FORMAT is a series, deliver (i) the SEASON spine using the
grid above at season level, then (ii) the PILOT episode using the same grid
at episode level, then (iii) a one-line engine statement: what generates a
new episode every week.

Here is my premise / synopsis:
{{SYNOPSIS_OR_PREMISE}}

Format: {{FORMAT}}
Genre and tone: {{GENRE_TONE}}
```

## Variables
- `{{SYNOPSIS_OR_PREMISE}}` — paste your logline + a paragraph, or a synopsis.
- `{{FORMAT}}` — e.g. "feature film" / "8-episode drama series" / "pilot episode".
- `{{GENRE_TONE}}` — e.g. "crime thriller, cold, procedural with moral rot".

## Example input
> *Logline: a disgraced archivist is hired to authenticate a dictator's lost
> diary, only to find it proves her own father was the regime's censor.*

## Notes
- **Every beat must turn.** If a sequence ends in the same state it began,
  it's exposition — cut it or fuse it into a beat that moves.
- The **midpoint** is where most drafts die. It is not "more plot"; it is a
  reversal that *changes the meaning* of what came before.
- The **Stakes Ladder** is your lie detector. Flat stakes = flat middle act.
- For series: the pilot's final image should open a *door*, not close one —
  the series engine lives in what the pilot leaves unresolved.
