# 01 — Logline & Premise

## Objective
Distil the story into **one irreversible sentence** and pressure-test whether
the premise actually has legs — irony, stakes, and an audience who cares.
A logline that could describe any film is not a logline; it's a genre label.

## When to use
At the very start, before you write a single scene — and again after the first
draft, because the story you *meant* and the story you *wrote* often disagree.
If the logline won't hold, the script won't either. Fix it here, not on page 60.

## The prompt
```
You are a script consultant and story editor working with a professional
screenwriter. I will give you a rough idea. Your job is NOT to write the
script. Your job is to forge a logline that is specific, irreversible and
sellable, then stress-test the premise behind it. Be ruthless about vagueness.

Do the following, in order:

1. THREE LOGLINE DRAFTS — write three different one-sentence loglines from
   three different angles (character-led, conflict-led, irony-led). Each MUST
   contain, explicitly or implicitly:
   - a PROTAGONIST with one defining trait,
   - an INCITING SITUATION,
   - a concrete GOAL,
   - the STAKES or ANTAGONISTIC FORCE.
   No proper nouns. No "a journey of self-discovery". Max ~35 words each.

2. PICK THE STRONGEST and explain in 2 lines *why* it wins (clarity + hook).

3. ELEMENT BREAKDOWN — for the chosen logline, label each component
   (protagonist / trait / inciting situation / goal / stakes) so the writer
   can see the machine.

4. PREMISE STRESS TEST — answer each with a concrete sentence, not "yes":
   - IRONY: what is the built-in contradiction or "the last person who should
     be in this situation" element?
   - STAKES: what is lost, concretely and visibly, if the protagonist fails?
   - QUESTIONS: list 3 questions the logline makes a stranger *need* answered.
   - ORIGINALITY vs COMPS: name 2 comparable titles and the ONE thing that
     makes this different from both.
   - AUDIENCE: who is this *for*, and why now?

5. KILL SWITCH — name the single weakest link in the premise and propose one
   sharper alternative. If you can't find a weakness, say so and explain why.

Here is my rough idea:
{{ROUGH_IDEA}}

Genre and tone: {{GENRE_TONE}}
```

## Variables
- `{{ROUGH_IDEA}}` — your raw idea; a few messy sentences are fine.
- `{{GENRE_TONE}}` — e.g. "historical drama, restrained, late-dictatorship Portugal".

## Example input
> *A state censor in a dictatorship is assigned to build the case against a
> banned novel — but he secretly loves forbidden books, and the trial starts
> to mirror his own life.*

## Notes
- **The "any film" test:** read your logline aloud and swap in another title.
  If it still fits, it's too vague — add the *specific* contradiction.
- Irony is the engine of hook. "A cop chases a killer" is flat; "a cop who
  can't remember his own face chases a killer wearing his face" has a hook.
- Stakes must be **visible on screen**. "Loses his soul" is invisible;
  "loses custody of his daughter on Friday" is a scene.
- Re-run this after the draft: the logline is your north star — if the script
  drifted, the logline tells you where.
