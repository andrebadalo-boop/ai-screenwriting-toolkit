# One-Page Synopsis — Template

> A one-page synopsis sells the **arc**, not the whole plot. The reader should
> finish it wanting the script — not feeling they've already read it.
> Fill each section; delete the *italic guidance* once replaced.
> Hard rule: **one page**. If it spills over, you are summarising scenes
> instead of telling the spine. Cut.

## The synopsis (the body — 3 to 4 paragraphs)

> Write in the **present tense**, **third person**, **active voice**.
> Name only the characters the reader must track (usually 3–4). Everyone else
> is "her brother", "the inspector", "the stranger". Proper nouns beyond the
> leads are noise on a first read.
> Never write "we see", "the camera shows", or "the audience feels". Just tell
> the story as if it's happening now.

**Paragraph 1 — The world & the wound (Act 1 setup).**
*Who is the protagonist *before* the story, and what is broken in their life
that they don't yet see? Establish the ordinary world and the flaw in action.
End this paragraph on the **inciting incident** — the event that cracks the
status quo.*

**Paragraph 2 — The chase & the cost (Act 2 rise).**
*What do they decide to do, and what stands in their way? Show 2–3 escalating
obstacles, not a list of events. Make the **price of failure** visible. Let the
relationship / B-story carry the theme in a line or two.*

**Paragraph 3 — The reversal (midpoint → all is lost).**
*The moment the meaning flips (false victory or false defeat), then the lowest
point where the old way is proven wrong. This is where most synopses go flat —
make the reversal change what we thought the story was about.*

**Paragraph 4 — The collision (climax) — and stop short.**
*Bring WANT and NEED to a head in the final confrontation. State the choice the
protagonist makes and what it costs. Then **end on a door, not a summary**:
leave one question, one image, or one unresolved thread that makes the reader
need the script. Do NOT tie every bow.*

---

## The theme (one line, after the body)
*What is this story *really* about, underneath the plot? State it as a tension
between two values (e.g. "loyalty vs truth", "memory vs survival"), not as a
moral. A theme that can't be argued against is a slogan — make it arguable.*

- [ ] The last line opens a door instead of closing the story?
